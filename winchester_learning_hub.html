A Disclosure and Barring Service (DBS) check is a background check required for all Scouts volunteers. It must be completed within 30 days of joining. The process runs alongside your mandatory learning — you don't need to wait for one before starting the other.

The steps below explain what happens and what you need to do at each stage.

1
Complete your SMS profile and notify your GLV/Team Leader
Once you've signed the declaration and filled in your profile on the Membership System, let your GLV or Team Leader know. This is what triggers the DBS process — it can't begin until your profile is complete.
2
Automated DBS email sent to you
Once triggered, the Membership System automatically sends you an email with a link to the DBS application. Check your spam folder if you don't see it within a few hours.
3
Complete your DBS application online
Follow the link in the email to fill in your DBS information. You'll need your address history for the past five years, details of any name changes, and three forms of ID. These are usually a passport, driving licence, and birth or marriage certificate, but many other options are accepted — typically at least one document showing your current address. Once done, notify your GLV or Team Leader.
4
ID verification by the DBS Administrator
A DBS Administrator will need to verify your identity in person. They'll check the original documents you entered into the system in the previous step. Your GLV or Team Leader will help arrange this.
5
DBS check processed — usually 1–2 weeks
Once submitted and verified, the DBS check is processed nationally. This normally takes between one week and ten days.
6
You'll be notified when it's complete
You will receive notification when the DBS check is complete and your record in the Membership System will be updated automatically.

Helper
Not registered on SMS · No DBS check
Informal

A Helper is anyone who assists at a Scouts session without being formally registered. This is typically a parent or carer who turns up occasionally to lend a hand. Helpers are not DBS checked and are not members of Scouts.

✓ Attend and help at meetings
✓ Run activities under supervision of a Team Member or Team Leader
✗ Be left unsupervised with young people
✗ Attend overnight events
✗ Be put on a regular adult rota
If a Helper is coming along regularly, they should be added to SMS as an NMND or encouraged to become a Team Member — whichever suits their level of involvement.

🪪
Non-Member — Needs Disclosure (NMND)
Registered on SMS · DBS checked · Not a Scout member
NMND

Formerly known as the Occasional Helper, the NMND role is for adults — typically parents or carers — who help out on a regular or more meaningful basis. They are added to SMS by a GLV or Team Leader, which triggers a DBS check. They are not members of Scouts and do not go through the full volunteer joining journey.

✓ Help unsupervised with young people (once DBS is complete)
✓ Help up to once a week, or four times in any 30-day period
✓ Attend overnight events and Nights Away
✗ Access personal data about young people or adults
✗ Take on formal team responsibilities
✗ Count time towards length-of-service awards
✗ Receive Scouts membership benefits (e.g. personal accident insurance)
Winchester District: NMNDs who attend regularly or come on overnight camps are expected to complete the Safety and Safeguarding online learning modules. The NMND role should not be used as a way to avoid the full joining journey — if someone is helping regularly, they should be encouraged to become a Team Member.

Team Member
Full Scout volunteer · Completed joining journey
Member

A Team Member is a full Scout volunteer who has completed the volunteer joining journey. They plan and deliver the programme for young people and are a core part of the section team. Team Members can volunteer flexibly — some give time every week, others less frequently — and share tasks in a way that suits everyone.

✓ Plan and deliver sessions, activities and events
✓ Work unsupervised with young people
✓ Attend overnight events and Nights Away
✓ Access personal data relevant to their role
✓ Full Scouts membership benefits including personal accident insurance
✓ Time counts towards length-of-service awards
To become a Team Member, a volunteer completes the full joining journey — including DBS check, Safety, Safeguarding, Growing Roots learning, First Response, and a Welcome Conversation.
↗ POR 16.1.3 — The volunteer joining journey ↗ scouts.org.uk — Team Members and helpers
🌟
Team Leader
Experienced Team Member · Recognition of leadership in practice
Leader

A Team Leader is an experienced Team Member who has naturally taken on a coordinating and leadership role within the team. In most cases, someone becoming a Team Leader is already doing much of this work — the role is a recognition of that contribution rather than a new set of duties landing on them all at once.

If you're finding yourself regularly organising the session, supporting newer volunteers, and being the main point of contact with the GLV — you may already be acting as a Team Leader.

✓ All Team Member responsibilities
✓ Conduct Welcome Conversations for new volunteers joining the team
✓ Add new members to SMS and manage roles
✓ Support and line-manage other Team Members
✓ Liaise with the GLV on behalf of the team
Additional learning: Team Leaders are encouraged to complete Leading Scout Volunteers (see the Additional Learning tab) to develop their confidence in supporting and motivating their team.
