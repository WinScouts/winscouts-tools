What is Branching Out?

Optional learning you can do after your first year

Once you've completed your Growing Roots learning and settled into your role — usually after about a year — you may want to develop your skills further. Branching Out is a collection of optional learning modules that let you do exactly that.

You can complete as many or as few modules as you like, in any order, with no time limit. There's no commitment required — you don't have to be working towards a Wood Badge to benefit from Branching Out learning. Some modules are also required for specific permits and accreditations.

💻
Online via My Learning
Some modules are available as eLearning you can complete in your own time through the TSA's My Learning platform. Look under "Explore Learning" once you're signed in.
🤝
Locally via Hampshire Scouts
Many modules are delivered face-to-face or through workbooks. Hampshire Scouts runs regular courses — check the training calendar for what's available and how to register.
Important: Training helps but doesn't complete the job on its own. Each module has specific validation requirements — check these before deciding whether you need to attend a course. You may already have the evidence needed from your regular Scouting.

The Wood Badge

An international award recognising your commitment to learning and development

The Wood Badge is an optional award you can earn by completing a specific set of Branching Out modules. It has a history stretching back to 1919, when Baden Powell gave wooden beads from the King of the Zulu nation to volunteers who had completed their training — making it one of Scouting's oldest and most recognised awards.

Completing the Wood Badge is entirely optional. There is one Wood Badge, but the modules required depend on your role — whether you're focused on programme delivery (sections) or on leading and supporting other volunteers.

Note on transition: The Scout Association is currently developing new Branching Out learning. Some of the modules required for the Wood Badge are from the previous Adult Training Scheme and are continuing temporarily. The core process and award remain the same.

How to earn your Wood Badge

1
Assign a Wood Badge in My Learning
Log in to scouts.org.uk and assign either the Section or Manager & Supporter Wood Badge to your profile. Choose the track that matches your role. Follow the steps below, or use the direct link.
How to assign your Wood Badge:
1. Sign in at scouts.org.uk and click My Learning
2. On the My Overview page, click Wood Badges and skills (on mobile, tap the menu button top-right first)
3. Click the Wood Badges tab
4. Click the + button
5. On the Wood Badge assignment page, click + next to the track you want
6. Click Assign Wood Badge — a confirmation message will appear
↗ Assign Wood Badge ↗ My Learning guide (FAQ)
2
Review each module's validation requirements
Each module has specific things you need to do or demonstrate. Check these first — you may already have evidence from your regular Scouting or from work and life experience.
3
Complete the learning and gather evidence
Attend training if needed, or work through the validation requirements in your section. Upload evidence to My Learning as you go.
4
Request assessment through My Learning
Once you've completed each module's requirements, request assessment. A Learning Assessor will review your evidence and credit the skill to your profile.
5
Wood Badge awarded automatically
When all modules are complete, your Wood Badge is recorded automatically in My Learning and My Membership. A certificate and physical Wood Badge beads are sent via your District.
Winchester District Learning Assessors: All modules must be validated by a Learning Assessor. Winchester District has several — visit the Winchester Accreditations Dashboard and search for Learning Assessor to find the right person for your group.

Section Wood Badge Modules

What each module involves and how validation works  ·  Complete in any order

Pragmatic recognition: If you have relevant skills from work or life, these can often count towards a module. For example, experience managing people at work may cover much of Module 9, or running sessions that develop skills in young people can evidence Module 18. Talk to a Learning Assessor about what you can bring — you may be closer than you think.
8
Skills of Leadership
Develop your understanding of leadership styles and how to apply them in a Scouting context — planning, leading activities, and developing leadership skills in the young people you work with.
Validation examples
  • Use a systematic planning tool for a section activity
  • Apply a leadership model and reflect on the outcome
  • Lead an event or activity and evaluate your approach
  • Run an activity specifically designed to develop leadership skills in young people
↗ View module
9
Working with Adults
Build skills in working alongside and communicating with other volunteers, parents, and carers — including how to support decision-making and represent your group at a wider level.
Validation examples
  • Represent your group at a District meeting
  • Discuss where you've helped others make decisions
  • Reflect on your use of verbal and non-verbal communications
↗ View module
13
Growing the Section
Understand how to grow and develop your section — from recruiting new members and welcoming young people moving up, to contributing to the wider development of Scouting in your area.
Validation examples
  • Contribute to a recruitment event
  • Produce and implement a section development plan
  • Help with the termly moving-up or new joiners process
↗ View module
16
Residential Experiences
Develop the skills and confidence to plan and run overnight and residential experiences for young people — from pack holidays to camps and expeditions.
Validation examples
  • Help organise a Nights Away event
  • Complete the Residential Experiences quiz
  • Discuss your experience and planning with a Training Advisor
↗ View module
18
Practical Skills
Develop and demonstrate practical Scouting skills — and the ability to teach those skills to young people in your section.
Validation examples
  • Develop a practical skill relevant to your section
  • Instruct young people in two practical skills over a programme term
↗ View module
19
International
Bring an international dimension to your section's programme — helping young people develop a broader understanding of the world and Scouting's global movement.
Validation examples
  • Review your termly plan to check it includes international elements
  • Run two internationally themed sessions with your section
