📈
25 years of growth
From 407 youth members in 2001 to a peak of 1,547 in 2023 — a 280% increase driven by new sections and a broader programme.
🦠
Covid impact and recovery
2021 saw a sharp drop to 1,266 — a loss of 226 members from 2020. The district recovered strongly through 2022–23.
🐿
Squirrels momentum
Squirrels launched with 18 members in 2022 and grew to 99 by 2025 across 6 dreys. Demand continues with 76 on waiting lists.
⚠️
Recent slight decline
After the 2023 peak, youth numbers have edged down — 1,458 in 2025 and a partial 1,404 for 2026. Worth monitoring.

Role restructuring effect
Adult numbers jumped sharply in 2017 (from 276 to 487) when the new membership structure changed how roles were counted, particularly Support roles.

Governance declining
Governance roles (trustees, chairs) have dropped from 93 in 2018 to 57 in 2025 — a 39% fall. Trustee recruitment may need attention.

Ethnicity

ONS 2021 Census ward population ethnicity compared with Winchester District Scout membership ethnicity (2025 census returns, youth members only).

📊
District is predominantly White British
86.4% of Scout members with recorded ethnicity identify as White (all categories). The ONS ward data shows most wards are 89–97% White, with city-centre wards slightly more diverse.
🔍
5.5% prefer not to say
78 members (5.5%) chose not to disclose their ethnicity. These are excluded from percentage calculations. Data is from 2025 section returns — some sections returned all zeros, suggesting incomplete data entry.
🌐
Scout diversity broadly reflects local population
Non-white membership at ~8% is broadly in line with the Winchester district average (~7–10% depending on ward). City-centre wards (St Michael, St Paul, Badger Farm) are more diverse, suggesting further outreach opportunity.
